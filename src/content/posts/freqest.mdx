abstract: "We have demonstrated a noise-resilient way to detect extremely small motions of a single point of light without relying on high-resolution images. 
We adopted the idea of spatial-mode demultiplexing (SPADE), proposed by Tsang et al., and used it to concentrate the motion-related information while suppressing background noise. 
Instead of spreading light over many pixels, our approach measures it through two carefully designed spatial-modes that respond strongly to motion but only weakly to noise. 
Our method allows us to reveal oscillation frequencies that conventional imaging would fail to detect under the same conditions. 
Guided by a general theoretical framework, we show that this approach is theoretically near-optimal for extracting micro-motion information. 
Our method offers a promising direction for tracking weak signals in areas such as biological microscopy and astronomy."

## Introduction

Classical direct camera imaging is theoretically optimal in a noiseless universe; it reaches the quantum limit--imposed by the quantum Cram&eacute;r-Rao bound--for tracking micro-motions.

However, real-world detection is inevitably plagued by noise.
In practical scenarios, traditional imaging faces a critical limitation: distributing photons across many pixels severely reduces the photon counts per pixel, making the measurement highly sensitive to background noise.

Our work shows the possibility of overcoming this limitation.
By utilizing two elaborately designed modes, we alleviate this inherent contradiction of direct imaging, allowing deterministic micro-motion parameter estimation to achieve near-optimal precision even under extreme noise conditions.

## Quantum parameter estimation

The theory of [quantum parameter estimation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_metrology) was first started by [C. W. Helstrom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carl_W._Helstrom). 
Just like classical estimation theory has the [Cram&eacute;r-Rao bound](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cram%C3%A9r%E2%80%93Rao_bound) and classical [Fisher information](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fisher_information), Helstrom created their quantum versions: the [quantum Cram&eacute;r-Rao bound](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_Cram%C3%A9r%E2%80%93Rao_bound) and [quantum Fisher information](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_Fisher_information). 
Later in 1994, the [S. L. Braunstein](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Samuel_L._Braunstein) and [C. M. Caves](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carlton_M._Caves) proved that quantum Fisher information is simply the maximum possible classical Fisher information you can get across all possible measurement methods.
Together, these ideas build the basic framework of quantum estimation.

Here is how they differ: Quantum Fisher information lives on the quantum state itself. 
It tells you the absolute maximum amount of parameter information hidden inside that state. 
On the other hand, classical Fisher information is based on [probability distributions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Probability_distribution). 
It sets the ultimate limit on how small your estimation error can get, no matter what specific [estimator](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Estimator) you choose. 

As quantum mechanics developed, [quantum measurement](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Quantum_measurement) became a core concept in precision tracking. 
According to [Born rule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Born_rule), measurement outcomes always show up as probabilities. 
Because of this, we can think of classical Fisher information as being tied directly to your chosen measurement setup. 
Once you fix your quantum state and your measurement scheme, the classical Fisher information is locked in.
